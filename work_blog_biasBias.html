Background

Behavioural economics is gaining considerable traction in Government departments across the world as well as the private sector. There are now over 300 Behavioural Science units across the world and the number is growing (See the full listing by the OECD). Behavioural economics emerged as the marriage between economics and the cognitive sciences. Behavioural economics applies psychological and cognitive descriptions of behaviour to normative models to explain departures from axiomatic (self-evident or unquestioning) principles. Unfortunately many of the economic models assume we are self-interested, consistently rational, utility maximising individuals, but this just does not seem to be the case. Instead empirical research shows that these models do not always apply and the reason for this is cognitive biases and heuristics which provide the psychological explanation for these model failures. As a result biases have become part and parcel of behavioural economics.

Biases are systematic errors in information processing that can lead to sub-optimal outcomes. Identification of biases have helped tremendously in explaining why individuals do not always make optimal choices. The introduction of psychological mechanisms to economic theory has been shown to greatly improve our understanding of behaviour and decision making. For example, researchers have been able to reduce NHS missed appointment (), encourage savings (), help investors to choose portfolios that provide safe for their risk tolerance (), and help people to make better decisions about their health ().

Cognitive biases

Human "irrationality"

Information processing theory provides a framework for understanding how the human brain handles information. Decision-making is an executive function, requiring constituent functions to work together and create some output. This theory suggests that cognitive processes can be understood in terms of how information is perceived, encoded, processed, as well as storage and retrieved, akin to a computer system. The issues in processing occur due to limited processing capacity or where the assumptions of such processing are broken. Attention is a resource that allocates how information is attended to and processed, like any resource attention is a finite resource and paying attention to some stimuli or thought process means we must remove attention from elsewhere. For example, the act of having a very engaging conversation on a mobile phone in a car (even when hands-free) is the equivalent impairment of driving under the influence of alcohol (a blood-alcohol level of 0.07 - 0.1; Leung, Croft, Jackson, Howard & McKenzie, 2012). Cognitive biases stem from underlying systematic errors in the way information is processed, this may be bottom-up through specific mechanisms and processing, or this could be top down through limited scope or information and therefore we need to make decisions with limited, incomplete information. As a result, when we act in ways that go against our own interests, or may not be the most optimal choice, the resulting behaviour is often described through a cognitive bias, such as loss aversion, ambiguity aversion, mental accounting, and many others.
These all seem like systematic issues so why should researchers and practitioners not be using biases so much? The answer is that cognitive biases are not mechanisms, they are in fact descriptions of behaviour. A bias can not tell you what has happened and why, it cannot explain nor can it even produce behaviour.

Cognitive biases are not mechansism, they are in fact descriptions of behaviour

Practitioners and researchers should instead focus on mechanisms, which are the constituent processes that generate the behaviour. For example, in examining aversion behaviour, such as the so called ostrich effect where people seek to avoid knowing negative information. The ostritch effect merely describes the aversion, the mechanism is a operant conditioning (a form of conditioning where behaviour is shaped by their consequences) that is leading individuals away from the negative information. By referring directly to the mechanism at play, researchers and practitioners are able to be more specific and precise in their explanations.
