Tiered interest & Instant access savings accounts

How could tiered rates and multiple savings accounts minimise consumers' interest rates?

Author: Krishane Patel | 29/05/2024
Tag: Case Study

Summary

Discover the psychology behind tiered savings products and learn how maximizing deposits can help you accrue more interest. Explore how multiple accounts for different savings goals can impact your interest earnings.

Background

In today's financial landscape, it's crucial for consumers to maintain savings as a buffer and to secure their financial goals. Several types of savings products can support this, including:

  • Regular saver savings account
  • Instant/Easy access savings accounts (with flat interest rates)
  • Tiered interest savings accounts (easy access accounts with differing rates)
  • Fixed rate savings accounts

Each saving product have different benefits and drawbacks, however in this blog post we will focus on the Tiered interest saving accounts.


Tiered savings accounts offer varying interest rates based on the amount deposited. Most typically these accounts leverage higher interest rates for greater balances (though some exist that offer lower interest rates for higher balances). The more money you save, the higher the interest rate you can earn. This structure creates a powerful incentive for consumers to deposit as much as possible into a single account to reap the benefits of the highest interest tiers. The psychological mechanisms are incentive-based: higher deposits lead to higher returns, encouraging savers to consolidate funds to maximize interest earnings. The incremental increases in interest rates serve as a motivator for consumers to aim for the next tier, driving them to save more.

Tiered interest rates are designed to incentivize or disincentivize consumers to save more by offering different rates based on specific balance thresholds. For example, the table below shows a hypothetical tiered account where interest increases with higher balances.

Instant Access savings

Overview

Instant access savings accounts are savings vehicle designed to help provide customers with financial liquidity as well as securing a financial buffer. In giving immediate access to funds, customers are able to exert more psychological control of their finances.

Instant or Easy access savings accounts allow consumers immediate access to their funds, but this flexibility comes with lower interest rates compared to long-term savings products. The underlying interest rates are set by the Bank of England. Interest rates calculate the amount of additional money that is added to the account usually paid in monthly on an annual time frame. For example, assuming a 5% interest rate, a balance of £5000 would yield an interest of £250. With long term savings products creating compounding effects where for example the same interest rate over a two year period would produce £262.50 interest in the second year. This is because the second year provides an additional 5% for the 250 earnt in the first year.

Psychological mechanisms

Savings goals

Many retail banks offer customers the opportunity to allocate a given savings account for a financial goal. This has shown to be a significant motivator to help increase savings behaviour from consumers (Changwony, Campbell & Tabner, 2021; Soman & Zhao, 2011). Setting savings goals is valuable to customers because it provides a structured and organized approach to financial management. By clearly defining specific objectives, such as saving for a vacation, an emergency fund, or a major purchase, customers can better track their progress and stay motivated. Goal-setting features often come with tools and resources that help visualize the path to achieving these targets, such as automated transfers, progress tracking, and personalized savings plans. This not only enhances financial discipline but also helps customers build a stronger financial foundation by ensuring their money is purposefully allocated and their goals are systematically achieved by generating a reference position with clear trajectory through action planning (i.e. to save £600 in one month, means I need to save £150/week). Action planning along with evaluating consumption behaviour help customers to reach their savings goal (Patel, 2018).

Multiple accounts: A double edged sword

Although research shows that planning and goal-setting often lead to positive behavior changes, certain psychological mechanisms can reduce the effectiveness of these strategies. One of these is the problem of multiple competing financial categories.

When we create financial categories, we infer what is known as coherence. Where objects should not be applicable to more than one category. Though this seems quite straightforward and ideal, in financial contexts this has been empirically shown to lead individuals astray, and is referred to as mental accounting. Mental accounting is a psychological approach to monitoring resources identified by Richard Thaler (1999) where people categorise spendings for set categories such as bills (utilities), entertainment, petrol and so forth. Though this can be beneficial in certain situations by enabling goal-directed behaviour, it neglects the fungibility of money, £100 for fuel can just as easily pay for £100 of food. This can lead many people to mistakenly misallocate their resources.

In savings context, this could lead to a situation where a customer categorises their saving goals into discrete baskets and therefore distribute their funds according to some value or assigned weight. This means that the customer is no longer focused on the optimal returns in interest, but in the relative proportion that should be allocated to each pot and their respective interest earnt. This shift in attention means customers will resort to heuristics to allocate their savings, which are likely to be sub-optimal.

The Matching Heuristic

When customers are faced with multiple baskets, many individuals engage in a matching heuristic (Bernatzi & Thaler, 2001; Gathergood et al., 2019). The matching heuristic is a diversification principle, where they allocate resources matched to the percentage of the weight of their carchological goals. What does this mean in practice? Imagine you have two savings accounts, one for your holiday, one for a renovation for next year. As the renovation work is psychologically more important, you would allocate the proportion of funds based the relative importance. Say the renovation work was twice as important, then you would allocated 2/3 to the renovation account and 1/3 to the holiday account. This means distributing funds across multiple accounts rather than consolidating them into one. While this method helps in achieving specific savings goals, it can also lead to suboptimal financial outcomes. Particularly when instant access account hold tiered interest rates.

Niaive Diversification

"Don't put all your eggs in one basket" is a commone phrase used by many investors, which depicts the propensity to spread risk across investments. This spread principle is often referred to as the 1/n heuristic or niaive diversification (Bernatzi and Thaler 2001), which denotes the tendency to evenly allocate resources among the available options, regardless of whether the options are considered equal. Niaive diversification has been demonstrated in consumer choices by Simonson (1990) who showed that consumers choices fit the 1/n hueristic when purchasing the same product n-times simultaneously versus sequentially on different days.

Simulated experiment

To investigate how these psychological mechanisms impact product usage and choice behaviour, a simulated experiment was conducted testing the following with either two or three tiered instant access savings accounts:

  • Optimal allocation - consumer places all their funds into one account to maximise interest
  • Niaive diversification - consumer evenly allocates across 1/n accounts
  • Matching heuristic - consumer allocates based on a relative standardized weights (i.e. sum to one).

Method

A Monte Carlo approach was used to randomly generate allocation weights for each of the groups mentioned above. Optimal allocation was calculated by maximizing the expected return from placing all funds into one account. Niaive diversification was calculated as total savings balance/n. The matching heuristic was computed by randomly generating two or three numbers which were standardized to sum to one with some slight adjustment. The data was simulated for 1,000,000 individuals for each group, totalling 5,000,000 total, this is for the 2 allocation groups each for two or three accounts, along with the optimal savings behaviour. Savings data was generated using data from the ONS's Wealth and Assets Survey, which assumed the following parameters (25th percentile = £2100; 50th percentile = £12500; and 75th percentile = £58500; fitting to a log-normal distribution). Interest rates were gathered from reviewing retain banking sites such as Natwest, Lloyds Bank and even HSBC. The rates do not represent any one bank but are meant to depict potential market rates and their positioning (see Table 1 above).

Results

Analysis of accrued interest was evaluated with an OLS fixed effects model, regressing log-transformed interest on each group (using the optimal choice as the reference). The results demonstrated a significant reduction in interest compared to the optimal group. A family-wise analysis demonstrated significant lost interest for both diversification (β = -9.52%, p < .001) and matching heuristic (β = -7.57%, p < .001), compared to optimal baseline group. Furthermore, the difference the interest loss for diversification and matching was shown to be statistically significant using the linearHypothesis function from the car package (F(1,456.07) = 61.478, p < .001).

Sub group comparisons for each heuristic across different number of accounts was shown to demonstrate a significant loss of interest. Diversification for two accounts was shown to hold an estimated loss of 7.61% ;(β = -0.079, p < .001) whilst diversifying balances over three accounts showed further loss at 11.39% (β = -0.12, p < .001). The use of the matching heuristic demonstrates a better outcome but still a net loss of interest, with individuals matching across two accounts at 5.99% (β = -0.06, p < .001) and those with three accounts at 9.12% (β = -0.10, p < .001).

What is the impact for retail banks and customers?

For many customers the premise is that tiered interest rates in fact hurt your financial position, when you are holding more than one account. There may be many psychological incentives that could be at play including psychological control, where spreading more funds across accounts helps customers to feel they have more control over their spending. However the alternative may be true, where a recent study in Buy Now Pay Later (Maesen & Ang, 2024) demonstrated that the Buy Now Pay Later products reduces subjective financial constraints, leading the propensity to spending. A similar mechanism may be the case with reduced financial constriants maked customers feel more in control and tradeoff the lost interest for psychological control. account utilises tiered interest rates, and to allocate proportions of the balance to goals rather than seperate accounts.

For retail banks, the main takeaway is perhaps that such incentive mechanisms should be revisited, in light of the UK's financial regulator (the Financial Conduct Authority) new legislation on "Consumer Duty", which seeks to minimise customer harms through four key outcomes:

  • Price and fair value
  • Product and Services
  • Customer understanding
  • Customer support

This may impact retail banks across "Product and Services" as well as "Customer Understanding". Therefore retail banks should proactively communicate these insights to customers to help them make informed decisions about their savings. Advisory services could encourage customers to optimize their savings strategy rather than over-diversifying or using sub-optimal matching strategies that result in lower returns. If customers were to make this calculation and understand the impact, this may lost trust in their banks leading some customers to shift to other banks, which could reduce profitability.

Conclusion

The findings from this simulated experiment highlight a significant flaw in the design of tiered interest rate products when customers hold multiple accounts. While psychological mechanisms such as savings goals and the matching heuristic motivate consumers to allocate funds across various accounts, this behavior can result in suboptimal financial outcomes, particularly in reduced interest earnings. Diversifying funds over two or three accounts leads to a notable loss of interest, with customers losing up to 11.39% compared to the optimal strategy of consolidating savings. For retail banks, this has important implications in terms of customer education and product design, especially in light of new regulations such as the FCA's "Consumer Duty" initiative. Banks should reconsider how they communicate the impact of diversification and matching strategies on interest earnings to ensure transparency and build trust with customers. Providing tools and advisory services that help customers optimize their savings behavior could not only improve customer satisfaction but also protect banks' profitability in the long run.
