Research Interests

Decision making and behaviour change

People can often make decisions that seemingly go against their own interests. What makes an individual relapse in their diet? Why do people fail to save for their retirement? Why do people with chronic diseases fail to consistently take their medication? These behavioural artefacts point to a cognitive architecture that many researchers and practitioner call cognitive biases. For example the present bias is the description that people tend to demonstrate a preference for smaller, sooner rewards over larger later rewards. For example, how information and choices are presented to us can influence the decisions that we make. Understanding how these mechanisms operate can tell us not only how contexts influence our decisions, but also provide insights into how to modify and alter our behaviour. This has implications in real-world settings particularly in policy setting and in the private and third-party sector. This theme implements techniques such as lab experiments, computational models, as well as research in applied settings.
