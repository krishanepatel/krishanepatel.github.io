Tiered interest & Instant access savings accounts

How could tiered rates and multiple savings accounts minimize consumers' interest rates?

Author: Krishane Patel | 29/05/2024

Summary

Discover the psychology behind tiered savings products and learn how maximizing deposits can help you accrue more interest. Explore how multiple accounts for different savings goals can impact your interest earnings.

Background

Effectively managing your savings is key to achieving financial security and reaching your financial goals. In today's complex financial environment, there are various types of savings accounts to support customers, each with their own advantages and disadvantages. In this post, we delve into tiered interest savings accounts, exploring how they can be used to maximize interest, while shedding light on the psychological mechanisms that can influence your financial decisions, negatively impacting your savings outcomes.

Tiered savings accounts offer varying interest rates based on the amount deposited. Typically, these accounts offer higher interest rates for greater balances (though some exist that offer lower interest rates for higher balances), meaning the more money you save, the more you can earn. This structure creates a powerful incentive for consumers to try and put aside more each month into a savings account.

Psychological mechanisms

Savings goals

Many retail banks offer customers the opportunity to nominate their savings towards a financial goal. This has been shown to significantly promote savings for consumers (Changwony, Campbell & Tabner, 2021; Soman & Zhao, 2011). Setting savings goals is valuable to customers because it provides a structured and organized approach to financial management. By clearly defining specific objectives, such as saving for a vacation, an emergency fund, or a major purchase, this creates an outcome goal, to which they can identify a path and set of actions they need to take (action planning). For example, to save for my holiday, I need to save £2000 over a four month period (meaning I need £500/month). To do this I have to review my spending every week, identify areas to cut back on (such as takeaways) and put money aside into a savings account. Action planning has been empirically shown to help customers to reach their savings goal (Patel, 2018).

In most retail banks, customers can open multiple instant access savings accounts and can assign them to unique goals. The central idea is that would allow customers to gain a sense of control over their finances and build wealth.

Multiple accounts: A double edged sword

On the surface, opening multiple savings accounts for different goals seems like a practical and organized approach. Many financial institutions encourage consumers to open separate accounts for various objectives such as vacations, home renovations, or emergency funds. While this method offers psychological benefits, it can also lead to suboptimal financial outcomes due to how we perceive and manage our money.

When we create financial categories, we infer what is known as coherence, making sure that each category has little overlap with each other. For example we do not think of any overlap between types of cars and bicycles. In financial contexts, how we mentally categorise concepts has been empirically shown to lead individuals astray, and is referred to as mental accounting (Thaler, 1999). Mental accounting refers to the way individuals categorize and treat categories of using money, (for example buying and selling) as well as different types of spending (such as bills, work and even socialising). In reality, all money is interchangeable (the technical term is fungible).

For example, a person might have an account specifically for a holiday and another for a home renovation, treating the money in these accounts as entirely separate. While this may create a sense of control and structure, it can actually lead to poor financial outcomes. By splitting savings across accounts, consumers could miss out on opportunities to maximize their interest earnings when interest rates are tiered.

In savings context, this could lead to a situation where a customer focuses on how they categorize their saving goals rather than how they allocate money to optimise returns.

The Matching Heuristic

When customers are faced with multiple baskets, many individuals engage in a matching heuristic (Bernatzi & Thaler, 2001; Gathergood et al., 2019). The matching heuristic is a mental shortcut in which we allocate resources based on proportions rather than absolute amounts. For example Gathergood et al (2019) demonstrated that when repaying multiple credit cards, individuals do not prioritise the card with the highest interest rate, but instead repay both cards proportionally. Suppose a customer had two cards, card A with £500 credit and card B with £125 credit and they only have £100 to pay off these bills. The ideal strategy is to pay £100 off the card with the highest interest rate, however the researchers found that people would allocate the money proportionally, with 80% of the value to card A and 20% to card B.

What does this mean with savings accounts? Imagine you have two savings accounts, one for your holiday, one for a renovation for next year. As the renovation work is psychologically more important, you would allocate the proportion of funds based the relative importance. Say the renovation work was twice as important, then you would allocated 2/3 to the renovation account and 1/3 to the holiday account. This means distributing funds across multiple accounts rather than consolidating them into one. While this method helps in achieving specific savings goals, it can also lead to suboptimal financial outcomes. Particularly when instant access account hold tiered interest rates.

Naive Diversification

"Don't put all your eggs in one basket" is a common phrase used by many investors, which depicts the propensity to spread risk across investments. This spread principle is often referred to as the 1/n heuristic or naive diversification (Bernatzi and Thaler 2001), which denotes the tendency to evenly allocate resources among the available options, regardless of whether the options are considered equal. Naive diversification has been demonstrated in consumer choices by Simonson (1990) who showed that consumers choices fit the 1/N heuristic when purchasing the same product n-times simultaneously versus sequentially on different days. This is also a common investment strategy, where investors will balance the values of same-day purchase of same-day investments (Gathergood et al., 2023). The 1/N heuristic is a shortcut mental strategy to reduce risk and uncertainty by spreading resources evenly across buckets (regardless of whether these buckets have the same value or weight).

Simulated experiment

A simulation experiment was conducted to see how the matching heuristic and naive diversification could impact financial outcomes for retail customers holding multiple tiered interest rate accounts. A simulated experiment was ran testing the following with either two or three tiered instant access savings accounts:

  • Optimal allocation - consumer places all their funds into one account to maximize interest
  • Naive diversification - consumer spreads funds evenly across accounts.
  • Matching heuristic - consumer allocates based on a relative standardized weights (i.e. sum to one).

Method

Data was simulated for 1,000,000 customers holding two or three tiered interest accounts (which is the smallest number of accounts that would require customers to consider how to allocate savings). Their savings was simulated using data from the Office for National Statistics' Wealth and Assets Survey which showed the median savings (across cash ISA and instant access accounts) at £12500 [25th percentile = £2100; 75th percentile = £58500] and assumed to follow a log-normal distribution, where most individuals hold smaller balances.

Consumers then allocated these balances across two or three accounts using all three approaches: optimal strategy, naive diversification or a matching heuristic approach. Optimal allocation was calculated by maximizing the expected return from placing all funds into one account. Naive diversification was computed as 1/N where N refers to the number of accounts. The matching heuristic was computed by randomly generating two or three numbers which were standardized to sum to one. This created five different groups for each individual;

  • Optimal allocation
  • Naive diversification across 2 accounts
  • Naive diversification across 3 accounts
  • Matching heuristic across 2 accounts
  • Matching heuristic across 3 accounts
The data was simulated for 1,000,000 individuals for each group, totalling 5,000,000 total. Interest rates were created using ChatGPT 4o, gathered from reviewing UK retail banking interest rates. These rates do not represent any one bank but are meant to depict potential market rates and their positioning (see Table 1 below). All analysis was conducted on R using the glm function of lme4 package, and sandwich packages to report White's corrected robust standard errors.

Results

Analysis of accrued interest was evaluated with an OLS fixed effects model, regressing log-transformed interest on each group (using the optimal choice as the reference). Diversification or use of the matching-heuristic led to significant customer harm, with a 10% loss of interest for the diversification (β = -0.10%, p < .001) and 8% loss for the matching-heuristic (β = -0.08%, p < .001) compared to the optimal group. Furthermore, this 2% difference between diversification and matching-heuristic was shown statistically significant using the linearHypothesis function from the car package (F(1,456.07) = 61.478, p < .001).

Sub-group comparisons for each heuristic across different number of accounts was shown to demonstrate a significant loss of interest. Diversification for two accounts was shown to hold an estimated loss of 7.61% (β = -0.079, p < .001); whilst diversifying balances over three accounts showed further loss at 11.39% (β = -0.12, p < .001). The use of the matching heuristic demonstrates a better outcome but still a net loss of interest, with individuals matching across two accounts at 5.99% (β = -0.06, p < .001) and those with three accounts at 9.12% (β = -0.10, p < .001).

What is the impact for customers & retail banks?

Customers

When customers are given the opportunity to spread savings across multiple tiered-interest saving products, they experience a significant loss of interest. The matching heuristic provides a marginally better outcome than a naive diversification, meaning if customers are forced to allocate funds across accounts, it would be in their interest to weight these allocations differently. However both naive diversification and matching incurred a financial harm to customers. The presence of multiple accounts provides an innocent but misdirected focus on how funds are allocated rather than the overall interest accrued. As a result many customers may lose out when using multiple instant access tiered savings accounts. Although these differences are statistically significant, at the individual level these likely do not encapsulate large savings, however at the population level, this could lead to large scale financial harms, particularly within those facing more financially hardship or lower financial capability. Let’s consider a hypothetical retail bank with 500, 000 customers, where 40% of these customers use tiered interest accounts. If each customer had an average balance of £5,500, the total savings would amount to £1.1 billion across all accounts. If 50% of these customers employed suboptimal strategies like naive diversification or matching heuristics, and lost an average of 9% interest, this translates into £49.5 million in lost interest annually to the bank’s customer base. A more conservative estimate of 10% of this hypothetical 500, 000 uses tiered interest account would incur a loss of £7.425 million if only 10% of those consumers implement suboptimal strategies.

Many customers will expect banks, rightly or wrongly, to act with integrity (doing what they say they will) and competence (doing things well). A side effect of the financial harm is the erosion of trust in the banking sector. Where customers are adopting mal-aligned allocation practices, the banks are able to benefit from the customer's inefficiency, heightening the asymmetry between the institution and the customer. This loss represents not just a missed opportunity for individual savers but a critical challenge for the bank itself. Customers who become aware of their lost potential earnings could lose trust in the bank, particularly in a financial landscape where consumers are increasingly informed and tech-savvy. This could lead to a rise in customer attrition as dissatisfied individuals shift their funds to competitors offering more transparent or optimized savings products.

Retail banks

To mitigate this, banks must take a proactive approach in educating their customers. Providing tools such as personalized savings calculators, advisory services, and automated recommendations can help customers optimize their savings strategies and avoid the pitfalls of over-diversification.

Customer understanding

Customer support

This may impact retail banks across Product and Services as well as Customer Understanding. Therefore, retail banks should proactively communicate these insights to customers to help them make informed decisions about their savings. Advisory services could encourage customers to optimize their savings strategy rather than over-diversifying or using sub-optimal matching strategies that result in lower returns. If customers were to make this calculation and understand the impact, this may lost trust in their banks leading some customers to shift to other banks, which could reduce profitability. Alternatively banks could change interest rates depending on how many accounts customers hold, i.e. using a diminishing tiered interest rate system where greater balances incur lower interest rates for customers with multiple accounts. Furthermore banks could look to help customers allocate proportions of the balance for different financial goals, finding a common ground between goal-setting and maximising interest.

Conclusion

The findings from this simulated experiment highlight a significant flaw in the design of tiered interest rate products when customers hold multiple accounts. While psychological mechanisms such as savings goals and the matching heuristic motivate consumers to allocate funds across various accounts, this behavior can result in suboptimal financial outcomes, particularly in reduced interest earnings. Diversifying funds over two or three accounts leads to a notable loss of interest, with customers losing up to 11.39% compared to the optimal strategy of consolidating savings. For retail banks, this has important implications in terms of customer education and product design, especially in light of new regulations such as the FCA's Consumer Duty initiative. Banks should reconsider how they communicate the impact of diversification and matching strategies on interest earnings to ensure transparency and build trust with customers. Providing tools and advisory services that help customers optimize their savings behavior could not only improve customer satisfaction but also protect banks' profitability in the long run.
